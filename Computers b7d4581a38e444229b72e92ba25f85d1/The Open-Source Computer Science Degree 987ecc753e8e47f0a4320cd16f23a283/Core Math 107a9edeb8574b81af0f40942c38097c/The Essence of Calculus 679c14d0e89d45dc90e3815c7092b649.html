  • 1. The Essence of Calculus
    In math, whenever you have a hard problem, it may be better to play around with the idea rather than trying to get to the answer directly
    Mathematicians don’t just care about finding the answer, but rather general problem-solving techniques
    • The Area of a circle:
      • First, think about the area of a circle with r = 3. You want to figure out why the area is πr^2. So you divide it up into a bunch of rings and if you could get the are of all these rings, and add them up, you’d have the are of the circle right?
      • So you open the ring, approximate it as a rectangle. Its length is 2πr, by the definition of π. The height is dr (depending on how many rings you used).
      • You realise that this is wrong, but for smaller and smaller choices of dr (thinner nd thinner rings), it would be correct, right?
    • The area under a graph:
      • So you take all these opened rings (rectangles) and up them next to each other. You notice that these form a triangle where each rectangle has height 2πr (r is the r for that particular ring) and width dr (you chose this)
      • So in essence, the area of those rings is the same as the area of that triangle, which is the same as the area under the curve 2πr . The area of this triangle is A = 1/2bh where b = 3 (=r) and h = 2 * π * 3 , so A = 1/2 * 3 * 2 * π * 3 = π * 3^2, or πr^2 for any other r.
      • This is the precise , not just approximate, answer for the A of the circle. Magical, right?
      • (Note: hence, the area of the circle is the integral of the circumference of the circle)
    • Hard problem → Sum of many small values → Area under a graph
      • Turns out this applies to many problems
    • The are under any graph, generally:
      • You might look at a graph like x^2 and notice that for any increase in the area under it, that change in area can be approximated by dA = dx * x^2. For smaller and smaller choices of dx, this approximation will be better and better.
      • At 3 for example, because dA = dx * x^2 , x^2 = dA / dx. So (A(3.001) - A(3)) / 0.001 = 3^2.
      • This is true everywhere, not just 3, and for any function f(x), not just x ^2.
      • As dx →, f(x) = dA / dx is defined as the ‘derivative’ of A (the area function).
    • The derivative of a function is hence defined as ratio of the change in the function’s value when the input is increased by a tiny nudge, and that tiny nudge on the inputs as that nudge approaches 0, in other words the difference in the values of the function when the input increases by a tiny amount divided by that tiny change in the input.
    • The significance of all this is that because finding the function to the area under a curve solves so many problems, if you know how to look at any function and find its derivative, you can also look at any function and reverse engineer it to find its area function (=integral)
  • 2. The paradox of the derivative
    • Thinking of derivative as an ‘instantaneous rate of change’ actually makes no sense (cannot have change in one instant
    • The car’s speedometer:
      • What does velocity actually mean? You cannot have a change at one moment? Then how come a velocity function only takes one point of time?
      • At any moment, you might imagine the car’s speedometer taking a very small change in time, say 0.01s, computing the change in distance in that time and using that as the velocity.
      • Let’s call the above dt and ds. Velocity is hence ds/dt. When looking at the function s(t), we notice that this is the same as the rise over run of that function on the interval dt.
      • This is very close to the actual derivative
    • The derivative is what the ratio of the change in a function’s value over a tiny nudge in the input and the nudge in the input approaches as said nudge approaches zero.
      • The slope of the tangent line
    • For a visual understanding, imagine secant lines of a curve approaching the tanglent line of the curve as dt approaches 0
    • The derivative is the best constant approximation for the change in a function’s value around a point.
    • When finding derivatives, we are finding the slopes of the tangent line towards which the derivative approaches, hence once we find it, we can say that it is the exact value of the derivative.

  • 5. What's so special about Euler's number e?
    • Think about the rate of change of the function 2^t. For every 1 unit increase of t, the function increases by the value of the function at that time(at t=3 it increases by 8=2^t etc).
    • You might conclude that the derivtive of 2^t is itself. But that’s only when looking at ONE UNIT increases. For the derivative, we need to look at tiny changes.
    • Turns out, for every exponential function, the derivative is the function itself * some ‘mystery constant’, which comes from deriving from the definiton of the derivative da/dt = a^t * (a^dt-1)/dt when dt → 0.
      • This is the degree to which the derivative is proportional to the function itself
    • For 2^t this constant is 0.69.. and for 3^t it is 1.09..so we can see that somewhere in between this constant would be 1.
    • This number is e (2.7..). Every exponential function is proportional to itself by some constant. e is the number whose derivative is proportional to itself with the proportionality constant 1. This is how e is defined.
    • We can choose to write the proportionality constant of any function a^t in terms of e through some algebraic manipulation, as ln(a). This is just how we are choosing to write it.
    • It’s natural to write exponentials in terms of e as a^t = e^ln(a)t because we get to easily see the degree to which the function and its rate of change are proportional
      • Many natural phenomena grow in a way that is proportional to themselves (populations, money etc) so it’s useful to see the degree of this proportionality
  • 6. Implicit differentiation, what's going on here?
    • Think about taking the derivative of a circle x^2 + y^2 = 5^2. (slope of the tangent line to the circle). X not a function of y, though, they are just interdependent values related by this function. So, what do we do?
      • We can reason geometrically that the slope is indeed y/x, but how can this be generalised?
      • (the derivative function will be 2xdx + 2ydy = 0. Then the goal is usually to find dy/dx)
    • Think about x^2 + y^2 as a function S(x, y) of 2 variables. When differentiating x^2 + y^2, we are thinking about a tiny nudge to both inputs x and y, and looking at how much S(x, y) changes (dS) for this tiny nudge (any move in any direction, ie any dy and dx we choose).
    • For the circle, we are just saying that dS should be 0. it is the condition for which the tiny step dx and dy stays on the circle.
      • More precisely, this condition keeps us on the tangent line, not the circle itself
  • 7. Limits, L'Hôpital's rule, and epsilon delta definitions
    • The derivative, formally: The derivative is what the ratio of the change in a function’s value over a tiny nudge in the input and the nudge in the input approaches as said nudge approaches zero. It is the best constant approximation for the rate of the function’s change at that time.
      • On notation: when using d (df, dx etc) we are assuming that the values are eventually going to approach zero. For concrete nudges, we use delta. The limit is hence built into expressions like df/dx.
    • Epsilon-Delta Definition of a Limit: a limit around x=a exists if for any range delta around a (on both sides, as small as you want) every output of the function is with a distance of epsilon of lim of f at x=a (on both sides)
    • L’Hopital’s rule: for any limit f(x)/g(x) at a point where both function’s would evaluate to 0, and hence the function is not defined, we can think of a tiny nudge to x, dx. After all, if we go just a tiny nudge away from this point, we can approximate what the limit is. At x+dx, the value of f(x) is its derivative (because we are moving by f’(x) away from where the function equals 0), and same for g. Hence, the ratio of the functions is the same as the ratio of its derivatives because with derivatives, we are looking at what happens when dx goes to 0.
  • 8. Integration and the fundamental theorem of calculus
    • Imagine sitting in a car and you wonder if there was a way to figure out your total distance travelled only looking at the speedometer and the time passed.
      • if velocity was constant, this would be easy.
      • it would also be easy if you travelled in discontinuous jumps of velocity, you could calculate the areas of resulting rectangles in v(t) and t space. But that’s impossible.
      • This is actually a good way to start approximating the distance function from the velocity function. Just choose a time interval dt, and approximate v(t) to the v at the start/end of that interval
      • now just make the dt between those discontinuous jumps of speed approach zero and when we sum these rectangles together, we get closer and closer to the area function under v(t), ie the s(t) function
      • When dt → 0, this is the precise, unapproximated area function, or integral of v(t)
    • The integral isn’t a function, it’s just an expression, think of it as a short-hand notation for the sum of all those rectangles
      • You always take the integral between some start point and some end point
    • You get the integral by subtracting some antiderivative at the endpoint from the same antiderivative at the startpoint (note that there are an infinite number of antiderivatives)
  • 9. What does area have to do with slope?
    • How to take the average of a continuous function, ie with infinitely many values?
    • Often when you need to add together an infinite number of values, the key will be to use an integral
    • First, you could think about approximating the average through taking some finite number of values in the interval we want to average, adding them together, and dividing by the number of them
      • This may look like taking ain integral, except instead of adding together f(x)*dx values, you add together just f(x) values
      • But you can actually write the sum of f(x) in terms of dx. the number of those values will be (b-a)/dx where [a,b] is the interval in which we are taking the average. hence, the average will be f(x)*dx/(b-a)
      • In other words, the average will be the area under the curve on the interval (=the integral) divided by the width of the interval.
    • In general, whenever you want to take the average of an infinitely large number of items, see if you can rephrase the question in terms of integrals.
  • 11. Taylor series
    • A Taylor series, turns out, is a very useful way to approximate many functions and make solving said functions easier.
    • Taylor series is about taking non-polynomial functions and approximating them using a polynomial functions around some input (eg x=0)
      • The reason why this is useful is because polynomials are much simpler to work with. Easier to differentiate, integrate etc
    • approximating cos(x) around x=0
      • how to find the best polynomial P(x)=c0+c1x+x2x2P(x)=c_0+c_1x+x_2x^2P(x)=c0​+c1​x+x2​x2﻿ to approximate cos(x) around x=0?
      • First, we’d probably want them to have the same value at x=0. Hence, set c_0 to cos(0)=1
      • next, we’d probably want the slope to be the same too, so set c_1 so that P’(x) = 0 at x=0. We get c_1 = 0
      • Similarly, choose c_2 so that cos’’(x) (= -1) = P’’(x) at x=0, we get c_2=-0.5
      • Turns out this is a REALLY good approximation around x = 0
    • We could just keep adding more terms to make our approximation even better
    • What we are doing here, is taking information about higher order derivatives of a function and translating it into an approximation of the values of the function near some point
    • Generally: take as many derivatives of f(x) as you like at x=0, and the coefficients of the Taylor polynomials in P(x) are those nth derivatives, divided by n factorial.
      • To take any other point b than x = 0, we would write (x-b) instead of x inside P(x)
    • For some functions, such as e^x, or sin and cos: when we extend the Taylor Series of f(x) infinitely, it converges to f(x). Ie, the full Taylor Series of f(x) equals f(x)
      • For others, like ln(x), it diverges. Hence, TS is only a good approximation within some ‘Radius of Convergence’ around the input we are approximating
